National Parks

The United States protects significant areas in the country with various designations such as national parks, nation forests, nation monuments, and national seashores. In fact there are 15 different designations that are used. However, the most recognized and prestigious of the designation is that of national park. There are currently 59 national parks (see the table below) that encompass more than 50 million acres. National parks must be established by an act of the United States Congress. The first national park, Yellowstone, was signed into law by President Ulysses S. Grant in 1872. The newest national park is Pinnacles National Park, upgraded from a national monument in 2013. Criteria for the selection of National Parks can include natural beauty, unique geological features, unusual ecosystems, and recreational opportunities.

Twenty-seven states have national parks, as do the territories of American Samoa and the United States Virgin Islands. California has the most (nine), followed by Alaska (eight), Utah (five), and Colorado (four). The largest national park is Wrangell-St. Elias in Alaska: at over 8 million acres, it is larger than each of the nine smallest states. The smallest park, at less than 6 thousand acres, is Hot Springs. Arkansas. The most-visited national park is Great Smoky Mountains in North Carolina and Tennessee, with over nine million visitors each year. In contrast, the remote parks in Alaska typically get 10 - 15 thousand visitors annually.
